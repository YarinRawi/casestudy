01The Problem

ELEM's work is powerful. Their website wasn't.

ELEM has been supporting at-risk youth in Israel since 1983. But their digital presence wasn't reflecting the weight or urgency of that work — and it was costing them donors and volunteers.

Problem

No emotional hook above the fold

The homepage opened with a navigation bar and text. The human story — the teen on the street, the volunteer who showed up — was buried below layers of institutional copy.

Problem

Donation CTA required too many steps

To donate, users had to find the link, navigate to a separate page, and work through a four-step form. Most dropped off before completing. The volunteer flow was even worse.

Solution

Lead with the human story

A split hero — photo on the left, action on the right — grounds the user in the reality of the work before asking anything of them. Two clear CTAs, above the fold, always.

02The Hero

One moment changes everything.

03Visual Language

Colour that works. Not colour that decorates.

Every colour choice is functional. Dark navy anchors trust. Orange drives action — and nothing else uses it. White space handles the rest.

04Page Architecture

Four pages. One clear hierarchy.

Homepage — Lead with impact

Split hero above the fold. Below: impact statistics, volunteer stories, donation amounts with real examples of what each funds. No generic "about us" opening.

Activities — What ELEM actually does

Each programme gets its own section: street outreach, drop-in centres, employment support, crisis intervention. Visual icons, clear descriptions, and a direct contact for each.

Donation — Friction removed

One-page donation flow. Choose amount (with real context — "₪50 buys supplies for one teen for a month"), choose frequency, checkout. Dropped from 4 steps to 2.

Volunteer — Make it easy to say yes

Clear roles, time commitments, and what to expect. A short interest form — not a 20-question application — that routes volunteers to the right programme coordinator.
